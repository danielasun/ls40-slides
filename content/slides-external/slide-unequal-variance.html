Comparing two groups

Up to now, when comparing two groups, we considered a null hypothesis relying on the two groups coming from the same population. The question we were answering was:
If two samples are drawn from the same population, what is the chance of finding a difference this large and larger?
The chance here is quantified by a p-value
Null Hypothesis
The Null Hypothesis we want to test is that the means, or medians (or any central descriptor of your choosing) are equal, assuming that the two groups A & B were sampled from the same underlying population p (can have any shape).
We could write that: $p(x) = p_A(x) = p_B(x)$
this implies that all the true statistics are assumed equal ($\bar{x}_A = \bar{x}_B$, $\mathrm{m}_A=\mathrm{m}_B$, etc..)
Formally, we write the Null hypothesis as Name: Hypothesis|Assumption, so:
$\mathrm{H}_0: \bar{x}_A = \bar{x}_B | p_A(x) = p_B(x)$

Comparing two groups

Student's T-Test
What we've learned so far is the resampling method equivalent to the Student's T-Test which tests the difference between the means of two groups, evaluating the null hypothesis that both samples were from the same underlying distribution (theory also assume a normal shaped population, N):
$\mathrm{H}_0: \bar{x}_A = \bar{x}_B | N(\mu_A, \sigma_A) = N(\mu_B, \sigma_B)$
it assumes A and B have the same "true variance". Thus it is called the equal variance t-test
Similar to our $d$ statistic (difference), the T-Test use a t statistic, which has a "standardized" form (normalized in term of SE).
$t=\frac{\bar{x}_A-\bar{x}_B}{\sqrt{\frac{\sigma_\mathrm{AB}}{n_1} + \frac{\sigma_\mathrm{AB}}{n_2}}}$
Where $\sigma_{AB}$ is the "pooled" standard deviation by combining two Normal distributions.

Comparing two groups

What if we just wanted to compare the centers of two distributions knowing full well that they come from different populations?
(When we say different populations, we usually mean that characteristics such as the variance or the skew are different).
if the two groups have very different variances, for example, it would not be reasonable to say they were sampled from the same population as they exist on two different scales.
The formulation of this Null Hypothesis would be: $\mathrm{H}_0: \bar{x}_A = \bar{x}_B | \fragapply{highlight-danger}{p_A(x) \neq p_B(x)}$
