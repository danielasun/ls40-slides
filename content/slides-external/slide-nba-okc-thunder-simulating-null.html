Can we find an association?

This difference of -35.5% between the conditional proportions (or similarly, the relative risk of 3.31), appears quite substantial.

What is the null hypothesis in this investigation?
Winning a home-game is as likely to occur in front of a sold-out crowd or not ($d=0$ or $RR=1$).
The observed difference in relative proportion of wins (or the high RR) could be explained by chance alone.

We are interested in investigating whether the presence of a sold-out crowd during a home-game is associated with a pattern of wins.
Alternative hypothesis: There is an association between whether the team plays its home-game in front of a sold-out crowd and the chance of winning a game ($d\neq 0$ or $RR\neq 1$).

Simulating the null

Our strategy will be to determine how surprising the observed result would be if the null hypothesis were true . We can determine this by using simulation to generate a large number of "could have been" results under that null hypothesis and examine the null distribution of our statistic.

If the observed result from our investigation falls in one of the tails of that distribution, then we have strong evidence against the null hypothesis (the probability of it to happen by random chance alone is very low).

In the actual data, there were 15 total wins by the OKC Thunnder during a home-game of the 2008-2009 season.
  • If there was no association of winning with the explanatory variable (presence or not of a sold-out crowd), then those wins would have occurred regardless of whether or not the stadium was full or not.
  • if this is true, then any differences we do see between the two groups arise solely from the randomness in the assignment to the explanatory variable groups.

What would be the probability of observing a difference in proportion of -0.355 if there was no association between a sold-out crowd and winning or losing?

The probability is very low (only $\approx$2.5% of the simulations had such an extreme difference in proportions in both directions).

We have strong evidences supporting the idea that it is less likely for the Oklahoma City Thunder to win when they play in front of a sell out crowd.

Possible explanations for home court disadvantage

There are three possible explanations for this odd finding that the team had a better winning percentage with smaller crowds:

Random chance (but we pretty much ruled it out)

The sell out crowd caused the Thunder to play worse, perhaps because of pressure or nervousness

The sell out crowd did not cause a worse performance, and some other issue (variable) explains why they had a worse winning percentage with sell out crowds

In other words, for #3, a third variable is at play, which is related to both the crowd size and the game outcome. (confounding variable)
