Relationship between a quantitative variable and a categorical variable:
The $MAD$ (Mean Absolute Difference in means/medians), "global" statistic to compare 3 means or more.

$\mathrm{MAD}=\frac{\mid d_1\mid + \mid d_2 \mid + \mid d_3 \mid}{3}$

$\mathrm{MAD}=\frac{\mid-3.25\mid+\mid-15.25\mid+\mid-18.5\mid}{3}$

$\mathrm{MAD}=12.33$

The average distance between one of the means to another is 12.33.

If the samples were heavily skewed, we could have calculated the MAD from the medians instead (with resampling statistics we are free to adapt our statistic of interest to the characteristics of our data).

Comparing means: going beyond the MAD

The MAD statistic is a simple generalization of what we used to test for a difference between two samples (difference between two groups).

Unfortunately, however, it is not a standardized statistic in that it does not convey information about the sample sizes involved. In addition, without the sampling distribution of the $MAD$, we don't really know how the value of the $MAD$ itself compares.

Note: the sample size is taken in account when computing the MAD from a dataset, but the value of the statistic itself does not render any information about the sample size.

Also, the MAD statistic does not lead easily to a simple shortcut based on theory (which some people can find annoying).
