Confidence intervals
Imagine we're interested in the parameter of a population.
The perfect world:
  • We have access to the full population dataset and we can calculate our parameter.
  • We can collect a large number of samples from the population and we compute the true sampling distribution of the corresponding statistic (from the CLT, we could estimate our parameter by calculating the mean of our sampling distribution).
The real world:
We could only collect one sample.
  • We can calculate the corresponding statistic, but we don't know how good of an estimate of the true parameter it is.
  • Using the Bootstrap method, we can compute a bootstrap sampling distribution from our sample to get an estimate of the variability of the true sampling distribution.
    We can define an interval within which we are confident the true parameter will lie for (1-$\alpha$)% of the samples we could have collected and constructed this interval from.
This interval is the confidence interval of our parameter.

Confidence intervals
Why will the true parameter be in the 95% confidence interval for 95% of the samples we computed the interval from?
  • From the normal distribution characteristics, we now that 95% of the samples statistics will lie within $\pm 1.96\times SE$ of the mean of the sampling distribution.
  • So the mean of the sampling distribution will lie within that same distance of 95% of the samples statistics we could compute.
  • If we collect a sample and calculate a statistic, if we can get an estimate of this interval, we know that 95% of the time, the true parameter will lie in this interval.
  • By computing the bootstrap sampling distribution from our sample, we can estimate what would be the variability of the true sampling distribution, and so we can estimate this confidence interval.
