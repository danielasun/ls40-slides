Metabolic rate in Northern fulmars

Two researchers, Dr. Furness and Dr. Bryant measured the resting metabolic rate for 8 males and 6 females breeding Northern fulmars.

Visually, we clearly see that there is a difference in the spread of the metabolic rate data they obtained from the males and females fulmars.

Are these medians different? Is $d$ significant statistically? Biologically?
Which strategy could we used to compare these two groups?

Metabolic rate in Northern fulmars

Remember, we are testing:

$\mathrm{H}_0: \bar{x}_A = \bar{x}_B | p_A(x) \neq p_B(x)$

Question: If only the medians of the two different populations were equal, what is the chance of seeing a difference as big and bigger than observed?

We simulate this by first setting the medians equal to each other (recentering of the data).
$X_A\leftarrow x_A - m_A\\~X_B\leftarrow x_B - m_B$
Thus, $m(X_A) = m(X_B) = 0$

Metabolic rate in Northern fulmars

Then we resample from each recentered data with replacement, keeping our populations separated, and compute our statistic, $d$ for each simulation.
We do not have strong evidence against the hypothesis of the metabolic rate of males and females fulmars having the same value.

Comparing two groups

Welch's T-Test
What we just seen is equivalent to what you'll hear being called the Welch's T-Test, which is a variance of the Student's T-Test dealing with the comparison of two groups with unequal variance:
$\mathrm{H}_0: \bar{x}_A = \bar{x}_B | N(\mu_A, \sigma_A) \neq N(\mu_B, \sigma_B)$
it does not assumes that A and B have the same "true variance" and such the variances are kept separated in the denominator when calculating the standardized Welch's t statistic.
$t=\frac{\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_B}{\sqrt{\frac{s_A^2}{n_A}+\frac{s_B^2}{n_B}}}$
