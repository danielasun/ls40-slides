The sample we calculated our statistic of interest from could have been drawn from population distributions with parameter $\pi$ ranging at least from 0.5 to 0.62 (we could not reject the null hypothesis at the two tailed 0.05 level for this range of values).

The long-run probability $\pi_\textrm{win}$ for UCLA to win a game against USC is in this range estimate.

Estimating $\pi_{\textrm{win}}$ for UCLA against USC

$\hat{p}$ 0.562
$\pi_0$ 0.48 0.5 0.55 0.58 0.6 0.62 0.65

Key ideas:

  • The interval of plausible values we just obtained approximates what is called the 95% confidence interval of the long-run probability for UCLA to win a game against USC. (we used $\alpha=0.05$ as statistical significance threshold)
  • The 95% value is called the confidence level, and is a measure of how confident we are about our interval containing the true population parameter.
  • If you think about it, we can also actually use the 95% confidence interval as a measure of the strength of evidence!
    if the parameter of the null distribution we're testing is in the interval, then we cannot reject that our sample has been drawn from such a null distribution.

Right now, we only have a rough idea of the boundaries of the 95% confidence interval, since we only tested a limited quantity of plausible values.

(for example, we only know that the lower limit of our confidence interval is somewhere between 0.48 and 0.50, we don't know its exact value).

We could test against other values. But this would be time consuming, and still limited to the values we would test.
