P-value
A p-value measures a sample's compatibility (statistics of interest/estimator) with a hypothesis (usually, the null hypothesis).
What would be the probability of observing this result if the null hypothesis was true?
A p-value does not give any indication about the truth of the hypothesis, this is not a measure of the probability of the hypothesis being true!
Main factors influencing a p-value:
$(\hat{p}-\pi_0)$
The further away the observed statistic (descriptor/estimator) is from the center of the null distribution, the more evidence there is against the null hypothesis.
Sample size
As the sample size increases (and the value of the observed statistic stays the same), the strength of evidence against the null hypothesis increases.
One- or two-sided test
Two-sided tests provide less evidence against the null hypothesis (simulation results "at least as extreme as" the initial observation considered on both sides).

Note on one- and two-sided tests

1 They answer different questions

Using a 1-sided test ($H_a: \pi_\textrm{win}>0.5$ for UCLA) we could conclude that UCLA was most likely better than USC.

However, if we consider a 2-sided alternative hypothesis ($H_a: \pi_\textrm{win}\neq0.5$ for UCLA), the resulting computed p-value doesn't provide strong evidence against the hypothesis that UCLA and USC have the same probability of winning a game.

you're investigating two different questions, so you may get two different results.

2 Critical limits in a 2-sided test

The nature of the chosen descriptor/estimator characterizing the initial result will dictate how you will calculate the critical limits to compute a two-sided p-value.

Proportion: $\hat{p}\in[0, 1]$. Use $\pi_0\pm\mid\hat{p}_0-\pi_0\mid$
Ratio (fold change): $r\in[0, \infty]$. Use $\frac{1}{r_0}$ and $r_0$ ($1\div\frac{1}{r_0}$)
e.g.: if UCLA had won 3 times as many games as USC, the opposite would be a third as many games as USC.
