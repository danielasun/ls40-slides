Sample with one categorical variable, $\geq2$ possible responses

How likely it is to observed such a distribution of frequencies of the counts for each category if the probability of observing each sign was the same
$H_0: \pi_\mathrm{rock}=\pi_\mathrm{paper}=\pi_\mathrm{scissors}=\frac{1}{3}$

Note: the probabilities for each possible response outcome don't have to be the same

Need a statistic of interest that characterizes the overall difference in distribution pattern of the frequencies (expected vs observed)

  • Mean Absolute Difference (MAD) of the proportions
  • $\chi^2$ (standardized statistic)

$\chi^2$ for goodness of fit

Characterize the discrepancy between the overall distribution pattern of observed and theoretical counts.

$\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{(\mathrm{Observed}_i-\mathrm{Expected}_i)^2}{\mathrm{Expected}_i}$

Mendel's pea experiment

  • Obsv. units: each pea
  • Variable: pea type
    (4 possible outcomes)

$\mathrm{Expected}_i = n_\mathrm{total}\times\pi_i$

Phenotype Observed count Observed proportion Theoretical proportion Theoretical count
Round, Yellow $315$ $315/556$ $9/16$ $315\times9/16=\texclass{pop}{312.75}$
Round, Green $108$ $108/556$ $3/16$ $108\times3/16=\texclass{pop}{104.25}$
Wrinkled, Yellow $101$ $101/556$ $3/16$ $101\times3/16=\texclass{pop}{104.25}$
Wrinkled, Green $32$ $32/556$ $1/16$ $32\times1/16=\texclass{pop}{34.75}$
Total $556$ $556/556$ $1$ $556\times1=\texclass{pop}{556}$

Use the counts, not the proportions to calculate the $\chi^2$!
The sample size information is included in the counts, not in the proportions ... a distribution of proportions $[1.0 / 0.0 / 0.0 / 0.0]$ means very different things if it is from a sample of size 1 as opposed to a sample of size 100)
